Abstract

On the internet, the number of choices is overwhelming, and we need effective information filtering systems to prioritize and personalize the information. Recommender systems deal with the problem of information overload by filtering vital information fragment out according to user's preferences, interest, or observed behavior about item. We want to know whether a particular user would prefer an item or not.

Recommender systems are important - they drive the revenues of E-commerce leaders like Amazon and Netflix, and add real values to customers. A variety of approaches for building recommender systems have been developed and achieved significant success, and this project aims at implementing a few popular models in the field to better understand their performances and the potential in recommending movies to particular customers.

Data and Approach

  • Approach

    In general, recommender systems are based on one of two strategies. The content filtering approach constructs a profile for each user (e.g., demographic information) or movie (e.g., genres). In associating similarities of profiles and learning user preferences, movies "similar" to user preferences can be recommended.

    The collaborative filtering relies only on past user bahavior (e.g., movie ratings), without requiring the creation of explicit profiles. It calculates similarity between items or users based on ratings. On the one hand, it can address data aspects that are often elusive and difficult to profile. External information for creating profiles might not be always available as well. On the other hand, it suffers from the "cold-start" problem due to its inability to address the system's new products and users.

    The two primariy areas of collaborative filtering are the neighborhood methods and latent factor models. Neighborhood methods compute the relationships between items or users. In the item-based approach, a movie's neighbors are those tend to get similar ratings by the same user. Item-based approach has better scalability and reasonings for explaining compared to user-based one.

    Latent factor models learns hidden features inferred from the ratings patterns. Matrix factorization is popular in recent years due to its good scalability and predictive accuracy. It allows incorporation of implicit feedback as the explicit feedback (ratings) often only comprises a sparse matrix, since any single user is likely to have rated only a small percentage of possible items.

  • Dataset

    MovieLens 100K Dataset, collected by the GroupLens Research Project at the University of Minnesota, consists of 100,000 ratings (1-5) from 943 users on 1682 movies. Each user has rated at least 20 movies, with users and items numbered consecutively from 1. Besides this full dataset, demographic information about the users, and information about the movies are also provided.

  • Models

    Under content filtering approach, Random Forest and Logistic Regression were performed by treating each rating as a training instance for prediction. Each instance has a single feature vector of 49 binary attributes. Under collaborative filtering approach, the Item-based neighborhood model and the Latent Factor Model using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) with Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) were developed.

Result and Discussion

The dataset was split into a training set and a testing set with exactly 10 ratings per user in the testing set. A total of 6 models were implemented in Python, covering algorithms discussed above. Each model is trained on the training set, and accuracies were measured by Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) on the testing set.
